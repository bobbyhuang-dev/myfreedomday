Efforts to eradicate modern slavery-a term encompassing forced labor, debt bondage, forced marriage, and human trafficking-have shifted from traditional criminal justice frameworks to complex, multi-sectoral global strategies. As of 2026, these actions are primarily organized around **Target 8.7 of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDG)**, which mandates the elimination of forced labor and modern slavery by 2030, and the cessation of child labor in all forms.

### **International Legal Frameworks and Governance**

The foundation of global intervention rests on the **Palermo Protocol (2000)** and various **International Labour Organization (ILO)** conventions, such as the Forced Labour Convention (No. 29) and its 2014 Protocol. These instruments provide a standardized legal definition that allows for cross-border cooperation.

A central driver of current progress is **Alliance 8.7**, a global partnership of 230+ organizations including governments, UN agencies, and civil society. The alliance utilizes "Pathfinder Countries"-nations like Malawi and Malaysia-that commit to accelerated timelines and innovative policy testing to serve as blueprints for global implementation.

### **The Shift to Supply Chain Transparency**

Recent years have seen a transition from voluntary corporate social responsibility to mandatory human rights due diligence. Significant legislative actions include:

- **EU Forced Labour Regulation:** Entering full effect in the coming years, this regulation prohibits products made with forced labor from being sold within or exported from the European Union, necessitating rigorous auditing of global supply chains.
- **UK Modern Slavery Act (and 2025/2026 NHS Updates):** New regulations specifically targeting public sector procurement (such as the NHS) now require mandatory modern slavery risk assessments before contracts are awarded, pushing the burden of proof onto suppliers.
- **Australian Modern Slavery Act:** This legislation mandates that large entities report publicly on the risks of modern slavery in their operations and the actions taken to address them.

### **Strategic Challenges and Emerging Trends**

Despite these frameworks, global actions face severe headwinds from geopolitical instability and migration crises.

- **Conflict and Humanitarian Settings:** Armed conflicts in Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and Sub-Saharan Africa have increased vulnerability to trafficking. Modern initiatives are increasingly integrating anti-slavery protocols into humanitarian aid and refugee processing.
- **Technological Integration:** Organizations are now leveraging blockchain for transparent supply chain tracking and satellite imagery to identify high-risk sites in isolated industries like brick making, mining, and commercial fishing.
- **The 2026-2036 Decade of Action:** The UN General Assembly has designated the upcoming decade to focus on reparations and addressing the historical structural inequalities that continue to drive vulnerability among marginalized populations.

Modern slavery remains a \$150 billion global industry. While legislative and technological tools have become more sophisticated, the "last mile" of eradication requires a fundamental shift in global economic incentives and the continued protection of worker rights in informal economies.
